Cannibalization in the energy market – what?

In an energy-only market, renewables bid close to zero marginal cost. That’s great for consumers, but once RES share grows, prices start collapsing exactly when the sun shines and wind blows.

  • Everybody wants clean energy → more RES capacity enters the market.
  • The more RES, the lower the electricity price during high-output hours.
  • New RES plants reduce earnings for all existing RES plants.
RES cannibalization in the energy market

Visualizing the effect

Think of a curve where the market value of RES (capture price) falls as RES share grows. At first it’s fine – then suddenly value erodes much faster than investors expected.

Add negative-price hours, and your LCOE spreadsheet stops making sense. That’s cannibalization.

If we ignore it, the RES build-out stalls.
Why it’s not “just a pricing quirk”

You think RES cannibalization is not that scary?

It is – because it attacks exactly the things policy-makers and system operators care about: investment appetite, system stability and meeting climate targets on time.

  • Investors lose interest in RES projects when revenue volatility explodes.
  • Grid instability rises – dispatch risk increases for everyone.
  • RES deployment targets look good on paper but become hard to finance.
  • We still lack storage and demand-side flexibility at scale.
RES should be good – not bad – for the energy market.

The real-world consequence

Volatility + congestion risk can cancel or delay projects.

When project IRRs fall below expectations, projects don’t just “adjust” – they get cancelled, delayed or quietly disappear from the pipeline.

That’s a risk not only for investors, but for security of supply and decarbonisation timelines.

What we’ve already tried

Existing market “solutions” – why they don’t fix cannibalization.

Over the last decade, we’ve stacked policy tools on top of energy-only markets. Many helped in the early days, but none were designed for a high-RES, cannibalization-prone system.

FiTs & FiPs – decoupled RES from market value.
CfDs – stabilised revenue, but deepen price suppression.
Capacity payments – increase system costs.
Curtailment – literally wastes renewable energy.
Priority dispatch – amplifies oversupply and instability.
Not working.
Real solution needed.

So what needs to change?

When capture price falls, business cases break.

Instead of pretending energy-only prices can carry all the risk, we need assets that absorb volatility and monetise flexibility – not just MWh.

That’s where storage, smarter market signals and new service business models come in.

Enter storage as a first-class market player.
Real solution

We need more storage in the market – not more band‑aids.

Our core thesis is simple: in high-RES systems, you don’t fix cannibalization with yet another support scheme. You fix it by adding assets that can shift energy in time and sell flexibility when the system needs it most.

  • Flexible market: storage that responds to real-time prices and system needs.
  • Dynamic pricing: signals that reward shifting consumption and charging patterns.
  • Smarter forecasting: combining RES output, congestion and balancing needs.
Storage turns cannibalization into an opportunity.
